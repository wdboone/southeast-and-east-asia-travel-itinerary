Destination Histories

Understanding the cultural context of each city

Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam

Ho Chi Minh City, formerly known as Saigon, is Vietnam's largest city and economic hub. Founded as a small fishing village in the 17th century, it grew into a major port city under French colonial rule from 1859 to 1954. The city was the capital of South Vietnam during the Vietnam War and was renamed Ho Chi Minh City in 1976 after reunification. Today, it blends French colonial architecture with modern skyscrapers, bustling markets, and rich cultural heritage. The city is known for its vibrant street life, historical landmarks like the Reunification Palace and War Remnants Museum, and serves as a gateway to exploring the Mekong Delta and Cu Chi Tunnels.

Hong Kong, China (SAR)

Hong Kong is a Special Administrative Region of China with a unique history and identity. Originally a fishing village, it was ceded to Britain in 1842 after the First Opium War and developed into a major trading port. For over 150 years, Hong Kong was a British colony, which shaped its legal system, architecture, and culture. It was returned to China in 1997 under the "one country, two systems" principle. Today, Hong Kong is a global financial center known for its stunning skyline, Victoria Harbour, vibrant food scene, and blend of Eastern and Western influences. The city features iconic attractions like Victoria Peak, Star Ferry, Temple Street Night Market, and Hong Kong Disneyland.

Macau, China (SAR)

Macau is a Special Administrative Region of China with a fascinating Portuguese heritage. Colonized by Portugal in 1557, it became the first and last European colony in China, maintaining Portuguese rule until 1999 when it was returned to China. This 442-year colonial period left an indelible mark on the city's architecture, cuisine, and culture. Known as the "Las Vegas of Asia," Macau is famous for its casinos, but it also boasts a UNESCO World Heritage-listed historic center featuring Portuguese colonial buildings, Chinese temples, and fusion cuisine. The Ruins of St. Paul's, Senado Square, and A-Ma Temple showcase this unique East-meets-West cultural blend.

Guangzhou, China

Guangzhou, the capital of Guangdong Province, is one of China's oldest and most important cities with over 2,200 years of history. As the starting point of the ancient Maritime Silk Road, it has been a major trading port since the 3rd century. During the Qing Dynasty, Guangzhou was the only Chinese port open to foreign trade for nearly a century. The city played a crucial role in modern Chinese history, serving as the birthplace of the 1911 Revolution that ended imperial rule. Today, Guangzhou is a bustling metropolis known for its Cantonese culture, world-class dim sum, the iconic Canton Tower, and a blend of ancient temples and ultramodern architecture.

Shenzhen, China

Shenzhen has one of the most remarkable transformation stories in modern history. In 1979, it was a small fishing village with a population of about 30,000. That year, China's leader Deng Xiaoping designated it as the country's first Special Economic Zone, launching China's "reform and opening up" policy. Within just four decades, Shenzhen exploded into a megacity of over 12 million people and became China's technology and innovation hub, often called "China's Silicon Valley." It's home to tech giants like Huawei, Tencent, and DJI. Despite its modernity, Shenzhen offers attractions like Window of the World, OCT-LOFT art district, and proximity to traditional Chinese culture.
